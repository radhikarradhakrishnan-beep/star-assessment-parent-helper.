# Understanding the STAR Assessment: A Guide for Parents

> **NOTE: This is synthetic, sample material created for a demonstration project. Student names, scores, and reports in this corpus are entirely fictional. For your own child's results, always rely on your school's official Renaissance reports and your child's teacher.**

## What the STAR Assessment is

STAR is a computer-adaptive assessment developed by Renaissance that many U.S. schools use to check how students are progressing in reading and math. Schools typically give it a few times a year (often fall, winter, and spring) to see where each student is and whether they are growing over time.

"Computer-adaptive" means the test adjusts itself to your child as they go. When a student answers a question correctly, the next question gets a little harder. When they miss one, the next gets a little easier. This lets the test find a student's level fairly quickly without asking hundreds of questions.

## The three main STAR tests

- **STAR Early Literacy** — used with younger students (roughly Pre-K through early elementary) to check foundational reading skills like letter sounds, phonics, and early comprehension.
- **STAR Reading** — used once students are reading independently to measure overall reading achievement.
- **STAR Math** — measures math achievement across number skills, operations, and other areas.

Your school decides which test your child takes, when they take it, and how the results are used.

## What STAR is — and is not

STAR is a **screening tool**. It is one useful data point, not a complete picture. It is **not** a diagnosis, and it is not meant to stand on its own. A single STAR score read in isolation is easy to misunderstand. The most reliable picture comes from looking at the whole report, your child's growth across testing windows, and your child's everyday classroom work — together with what the teacher observes.

Schools generally should not use STAR as the sole deciding factor for high-stakes decisions about a child, such as qualifying for special programs. It is one of several pieces of information.

## How often the test is given

Most schools test in seasonal "windows" — commonly fall, winter, and spring. Comparing a child's scores across these windows is often more meaningful than any single score, because it shows whether the child is growing.
