# Supporting Your Child After a STAR Assessment

> **NOTE: Synthetic sample material for a demonstration project. These are general suggestions, not personalized advice. Work with your child's teacher for a plan suited to your child.**

A STAR report is most useful when it leads to a conversation and a few concrete next steps. Here is how to act on the results constructively.

## Start with the teacher

The single most useful step is a short conversation with your child's teacher. Helpful questions to ask:

- How does this score compare to our school's benchmark and to my child's previous scores?
- Is my child growing at a healthy rate (what does the Student Growth Percentile suggest)?
- Are there specific skill areas to focus on?
- What support is available at school, and what can I do at home?

## Frame the results positively for your child

Children often sense how parents react to scores. STAR measures a moment in time, not your child's worth or ceiling. Emphasize growth and effort rather than a single number. A child who is "behind" on percentile can still be growing well — and growth is what compounds over time.

## If your child is at or above benchmark

Keep the momentum: maintain regular reading and math habits, offer gently challenging material, and let your child pursue topics they enjoy. The goal is continued growth, not pressure to stay at the top.

## If your child is "On Watch"

This is usually a monitor-and-support situation, not an alarm. Build consistent reading and math routines, celebrate small wins, and check in with the teacher about light-touch support. Often steady practice is enough to move a child back toward the benchmark.

## If your child is in "Intervention" or "Urgent Intervention"

This is a signal that your child needs more focused help now — not a judgment of ability. Partner closely with the school on an intervention plan, keep home practice short and encouraging rather than stressful, and track progress across the next testing windows. Many children make strong gains once the right support is in place.

## General habits that help

- **Read together daily**, even for short periods, and talk about what you read.
- **Make math part of everyday life** — cooking, shopping, games with numbers.
- **Keep practice low-stress.** Short, positive, consistent sessions beat long, anxious ones.
- **Watch growth over time**, not a single score. Compare each window to the last.

## What not to do

- Do not treat one score as a final verdict.
- Do not compare your child to siblings or classmates by percentile.
- Do not push test "cramming" — ongoing learning matters more than test-day prep.
