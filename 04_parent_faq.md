# STAR Assessment: Frequently Asked Questions for Parents

> **NOTE: Synthetic sample material for a demonstration project. For your child's specific situation, consult their teacher and your school's official reports.**

**What is a "good" STAR score?**
There is no single number that is universally "good." A good score depends on your child's grade, the testing window, the subject, your school's benchmark, and your child's own growth history. A Percentile Rank of 40 or above is commonly treated as "benchmark met," but the most useful answer comes from comparing your child to their own past scores and asking the teacher how the result compares to school expectations.

**My child's percentile dropped from fall to winter. Should I worry?**
Not necessarily, but it is worth a conversation. Percentile Rank compares your child to other students, so it can shift even when your child is learning. Look at the Scaled Score and the Student Growth Percentile too — if the Scaled Score went up, your child is still learning, even if their rank relative to peers moved. Ask the teacher for context.

**What does it mean if my child is labeled "On Watch" or "Intervention"?**
These are performance levels tied to score ranges. "On Watch" usually means slightly below the benchmark, where the school keeps an eye on progress. "Intervention" and "Urgent Intervention" mean the school recommends extra support. The exact cut scores are set by your district, so ask what benchmark your school uses and what support is available.

**Is the Grade Equivalent the grade my child should be working at?**
No. Grade Equivalent is a comparison to national norms, not a placement recommendation. A third-grader with a GE of 5.0 performed like an average fifth-grader *on this test* — it does not mean they should be doing fifth-grade work. Use it as a rough reference only.

**Can my child study or prepare for the STAR test?**
STAR is mainly designed to measure current achievement, not something to cram for. The best preparation is ongoing: regular reading and math practice, and familiarity with how the test works so test day feels comfortable. Talk to the teacher about helpful practice.

**Why did my child get questions that seemed too hard (or too easy)?**
STAR is adaptive, so difficulty adjusts as your child answers. Seeing some hard questions is normal and expected — the test is finding your child's level. Encountering challenging items does not mean your child did poorly.

**Where do I find my child's results?**
STAR results are usually available through the Renaissance system your school provides, or sent home as a family report. If you cannot find them, ask your child's teacher or the school office for a copy and an explanation.

**Should this score determine whether my child gets into a special program?**
STAR is one data point and generally should not be the sole factor in high-stakes decisions. If a placement decision is being made, ask what other information is being considered alongside the STAR results.
