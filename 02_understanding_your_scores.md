# Understanding Your Child's STAR Scores

> **NOTE: This is synthetic, sample material for a demonstration project. All examples use fictional numbers. For your own child's results, rely on your school's official reports and teacher.**

A STAR report shows several different scores. Each answers a different question. Parents often focus on just one number, but they make the most sense when read together.

## Scaled Score (SS)

The Scaled Score is usually the most important number for tracking growth. It is calculated from the difficulty of the questions your child saw and how many they answered correctly. On the unified scale, scaled scores range from roughly 0 to 1400 (STAR Early Literacy uses a narrower range, roughly 200 to 1100).

Because the same scale is used for every student regardless of grade, the Scaled Score lets you compare your child's performance **over time and across grades**. The key question it answers is: *Is my child growing from one testing window to the next?*

Tip: Do not compare Scaled Scores across different STAR products (for example, Reading versus Math) without guidance from the teacher — the scales are not interchangeable that way.

## Percentile Rank (PR)

The Percentile Rank ranges from 1 to 99 and compares your child to other students **in the same grade nationally**. A PR of 60 means your child scored as well as or better than 60% of students in their grade across the country.

Percentile Rank is context, not a judgment. A PR of 40 does not mean your child is "failing" — it means they performed better than 40% of students nationwide. It should always be read alongside the Scaled Score and growth.

## Grade Equivalent (GE)

The Grade Equivalent estimates the grade and month at which your child's performance matches the national average. For example, GE 4.5 means the child performed about as well as the average student in the fifth month of fourth grade.

GE is easy to misread. A fifth-grader with a GE of 7.2 is **not** ready for seventh-grade material — it simply means they performed well above the typical fifth-grade level on this test. GE is best used as a rough reference, read together with the other scores.

## Student Growth Percentile (SGP)

The Student Growth Percentile ranges from 1 to 99 and describes how much your child grew compared to **academic peers** — other students who started at a similar achievement level. An SGP of 65 means your child grew more than 65% of students who had a similar starting point. SGP is especially useful because a child who is below grade level can still show strong growth.

## Benchmark / Performance Level

Schools group scores into performance categories to show whether a student is on track. These categories are commonly tied to percentile ranges. A widely used set of labels is:

- **At/Above Benchmark** — on track; often defined as a Percentile Rank of 40 or higher.
- **On Watch** — slightly below the benchmark; the school monitors progress (often roughly PR 25–39).
- **Intervention** — below expectations; extra support is usually recommended (often roughly PR 10–24).
- **Urgent Intervention** — well below expectations; intensive support is usually recommended (often roughly PR below 10).

**Important:** the exact cut scores for these categories are set by each state, district, or school, so the ranges above are typical examples rather than fixed national rules. If you want to know the precise benchmark used for your child, ask the teacher or school.

## Domain / Skill Scores

Many reports break performance into specific skill areas (for example, in reading: vocabulary, comprehension, analyzing text). These domain scores can show relative strengths and areas to work on, but they are based on fewer questions than the overall score, so treat them as a guide rather than a precise measurement.
